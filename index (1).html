Conceptual Discovery

Core intuitions behind every exam topic. Queueing first, then inventory — matching exam order.

1. Queueing Theory

Core insight

Queues form even when average demand < average capacity. The culprit is variability — random fluctuations in both arrivals and service times create temporary imbalances that accumulate into waiting lines.

The Two Fundamental Laws

1st Law: Higher utilization → exponentially more waiting

As ρ approaches 100%, Lq explodes. Going from 80% to 90% utilization roughly doubles queue length. Going from 90% to 95% doubles it again. This is why you NEED idle capacity as a buffer against variability.

2nd Law: Higher variability → even more waiting at the same ρ

The Lq formula includes (CA² + CS²)/2. When CA=CS=1 (Poisson/exponential), this factor = 1. When variability is lower (e.g., CS=0.25), this factor drops to 0.53 — cutting queues nearly in half at the same utilization. Reducing variability is as powerful as adding capacity.

Pooling Principle

One queue feeding S servers always beats S separate queues

With pooling, when one server finishes early, it immediately helps the common queue. With separate queues, that idle capacity is wasted while another queue builds up. The math: pooled Lq uses exponent √(2(S+1)) which grows slower than S separate systems. Pooling is free performance — same capacity, less waiting.

Strategic trade-off

Pooling is operationally optimal but may not be strategically best. Separating queues (express lanes, priority classes, VIP lines) sacrifices efficiency for differentiation and willingness-to-pay segmentation. The exam tests whether you can argue BOTH sides.

The Strategic Triangle

Cost — Range — Service: pick two

No firm can simultaneously minimize cost (high ρ), offer maximum product range (high CVS from variety), and guarantee fast service (low Wq). Queueing theory formalizes this impossibility. Exam Part IV (strategic) often tests this tension.

2. Capacity Analysis

Little's Law: WIP = TH × TT

Universal. Requires NO assumptions about distributions. If you know any two of {items in system, throughput rate, time in system}, you can find the third. The exam uses this to convert between "how many kits are being processed" and "how long does each kit take."

Bottleneck identification

List every resource. For each: capacity = available time / time per unit. The resource with the LOWEST capacity relative to demand is the bottleneck. It determines system throughput. Adding capacity to non-bottleneck resources does NOT increase output. (Exam 2024 Part II: employees, scales, sterilization units — sterilization was bottleneck.)

3. Inventory Management

The Two Fundamental Decisions

How much to order? → EOQ (cycle stock)

Balances ordering cost (decreases with larger Q) against holding cost (increases with larger Q). At the optimum, the two cost curves cross. The total cost curve is FLAT around the optimum — a 10% error in Q only increases costs by ~0.5%. This means practical constraints (truck sizes, pallet quantities) can shift Q without significant penalty.

When to order? → ROP (safety stock)

Safety stock buffers against demand uncertainty during the vulnerable period (time between ordering and receiving). Without safety stock, you stock out ~50% of replenishment cycles. The key variables: service level (managerial choice), demand variability (σ), and vulnerable period length (depends on review type).

Review Types

Continuous review: VP = LT

You monitor inventory constantly and order the moment IP drops below ROP. You're only exposed to uncertainty during the lead time. Order quantity is fixed (Q = EOQ).

Periodic review: VP = LT + R

You check inventory every R days. You could miss a demand spike between reviews. So the vulnerable period is LONGER: LT + R. This requires MORE safety stock. The exam tests this distinction heavily — the 2024 exam used daily review (R=1), making VP = LT+1.

The #1 exam trap

Students forget to add R to the vulnerable period in periodic review. If LT=7 and R=1, VP=8 not 7. SS = z×σ×√8 not z×σ×√7. This single mistake cost many students 5+ points on the 2024 exam.

Newsvendor (Single-Period)

One ordering opportunity, uncertain demand, asymmetric costs

When you can only order once (seasonal goods, perishables, fashion), the optimal quantity depends entirely on the ratio Cu/(Cu+Co). This critical fractile IS the optimal service level — derived from economics, not chosen by a manager. High margin (Cu >> Co) → order aggressively. Low margin → order conservatively.

Formula connection

Q* = μ + z*σ is structurally identical to the safety stock formula ROP = D̄₁×VP + z×σ×√VP. The only difference: in safety stock, z comes from a chosen service level; in newsvendor, z comes from the cost-optimal critical fractile.

Supplier Comparison (2024 Exam Pattern)

Separate differential from non-differential costs

For each supplier: 1) Compute EOQ from differential costs only (ordering + cycle holding). 2) Compute SS from that supplier's lead time. 3) Add ALL costs (purchasing, transport, cycle holding, ordering, SS holding) for the total. 4) Compare totals. Purchasing cost differences typically dominate everything else — in 2024, Portugal saved €7.4M/yr vs. the US, mostly from the €200/box price difference.

Avg wait in queueWq = Lq / λ
Avg time in systemW = Wq + tS where tS = 1/μ
Avg # in systemL = Lq + ρ × S
Variablesλ = arrival rate μ = service rate per server S = # servers tA = 1/λ (avg inter-arrival time) tS = 1/μ (avg service time) CA = σA / tA (CV of arrivals) CS = σS / tS (CV of service) PCE = tS / W = 1 − Wq/W
CV quick rules

"Poisson arrivals" or "exponential inter-arrivals" → CA = 1. "Exponential service" → CS = 1. "Deterministic" / fixed → CV = 0. Given σ and mean → CV = σ/mean.

Queueing Gym

Ex 1: Customer Service — Separate Queues

Queueing▸

λ=15 customers/hr. 2 desks on different floors (no pooling possible). Fast rep: 5 min/customer (μ=12/hr). Slow rep: μ=8/hr. Customers assigned 50/50 randomly. Assume CA=CS=1.

Q1: System utilization? Q2: Expected # waiting? Q3: How to improve without more resources?

System utilization: ρ = 15/(12+8) = 75% But each desk sees λ=7.5/hr (50/50 split): Fast desk (S=1): ρ = 7.5/12 = 62.5% Lq = [0.625^√4 / (1-0.625)] × 1 = 0.625²/0.375 = 1.04 Wq = 1.04/7.5 = 8.3 min Slow desk (S=1): ρ = 7.5/8 = 93.75% ← danger zone! Lq = [0.9375^2 / 0.0625] × 1 = 14.06 Wq = 14.06/7.5 = 112.5 min ← nearly 2 hours! Total Lq = 1.04 + 14.06 = 15.1 people waiting Q3 — Improve without more resources: • Route more customers to fast server (60/40 or 70/30) • Ideally: common queue feeding both desks (pooling) • Cross-train slow rep to increase their speed

Ex 2: 24hr Pharmacy — Automation Impact

Queueing▸

λ=280 orders/day (σ=280). 5 employees: tS=25 min (σ=25). Machine alternative: tS=5 min (σ=5). 24hr operation.

Q1: Current Wq and tS? Q2: Impact of replacing employees with machine?

λ = 280/24 = 11.67/hr; CA ≈ 1 (σ_daily ≈ mean) Current: μ = 60/25 = 2.4/hr; S=5; capacity=12/hr ρ = 11.67/12 = 97.2% ← extremely high CS = 25/25 = 1 Lq is very large at 97% utilization → long waits Machine (S=1): μ = 60/5 = 12/hr; capacity=12/hr ρ = 11.67/12 = 97.2% ← same utilization! But S=1 with 97% is WORSE than S=5 at 97% The exponent √(2×2)=2 vs √(2×6)=3.46 Key insight: Same total capacity but different topology. 5 servers pooled >> 1 fast server at same ρ. The machine doesn't help unless capacity increases.

Ex 3: Hiring Decision — Cost Optimization

Queueing + Cost▸

λ=3/hr (Poisson). Hannah: μ=6/hr, $40/hr. Elaine: μ=7/hr, $x/hr. Waiting cost: $100/hr per case in system.

Q1: Total cost CH and CE? Q2: Max salary Elaine can request?

Both M/M/1 (Poisson arrivals, exponential service, S=1, CA=CS=1) Hannah: ρ = 3/6 = 0.5 Lq = 0.5²/(1-0.5) × 1 = 0.5 L = Lq + ρ = 0.5 + 0.5 = 1.0 CH = $40 + $100×1.0 = $140/hr Elaine: ρ = 3/7 = 0.429 Lq = 0.429²/(1-0.429) × 1 = 0.184/0.571 = 0.322 L = 0.322 + 0.429 = 0.751 CE = x + $100×0.751 = x + $75.10/hr Elaine wins if CE < CH: x + 75.10 < 140 → x < $64.90/hr

Ex 4: Express Checkout — Pooling vs Separation

Queueing + Strategy▸

λ=12/hr, 4 lines (separate S=1 queues, random split). tS=12 min, σ=12. 50% qualify for express: tS=5 min, σ=1 min.

Q1: Current performance? Q2: Express line performance? Q3: Should you implement?

Q1 — Current: each queue gets λ=3/hr, μ=5/hr, ρ=0.6, CS=1 Lq = 0.6²/0.4 × 1 = 0.9 per queue Wq = 0.9/3 = 18 min; W = 30 min Q2 — Express (1 dedicated line): λ=6/hr, μ=12/hr, ρ=0.5 CS = 1/5 = 0.2; Lq = 0.5²/0.5 × (1+0.04)/2 = 0.26 Wq = 0.26/6 hr = 2.6 min; W = 7.6 min ← huge improvement Regular (3 lines): λ=2/hr each tS_regular: 0.5×5 + 0.5×x = 12 → x = 19 min μ = 60/19 = 3.16/hr; ρ = 2/3.16 = 63.3% Wait time slightly higher for regular customers Q3 — Implement? Express customers: W drops from 30→8 min. Regular customers: W increases somewhat (19 min service). Net: likely yes if express customers are more price-sensitive. Need: data on margin per customer type to decide.

Ex 1: The Big Headache — EOQ Comparison

EOQ▸

D=3,000 cartons/yr. Subcontract: S=$1,000, v=$50. Prepackaged: S=$10, v=$60. i=30%.

Subcontract: EOQ = √(2×3000×1000/(50×0.30)) = √400,000 = 632 TC = √(2×3000×1000×50×0.30) + 3000×50 = 9,487 + 150,000 = $159,487 Prepackaged: EOQ = √(2×3000×10/(60×0.30)) = √3,333 = 58 TC = √(2×3000×10×60×0.30) + 3000×60 = 1,039 + 180,000 = $181,039 → Subcontract saves ~$21,500/yr (lower unit cost dominates)

Q1 — Continuous: EOQ = √(2×5000×10/(3×0.2)) = √166,667 ≈ 408 VP = LT = 3 wks; z(95%)=1.64 SS = 1.64×30×√3 = 85 bottles ROP = 100×3 + 85 = 385 bottles Q2 — Periodic (R ≈ 4 weeks based on EOQ): VP = LT+R = 3+4 = 7 weeks S = 100×7 + 1.64×30×√7 = 700 + 130 = 830 bottles

Ex 3: Frozen Delights — Full Inventory (8 parts)

EOQ + SS + ROP▸

D̄=80 L/day, σ=20, S=€200, v=€10, i=15%, LT=1 day, SL=99%. Current Q=800 (10-day supply).

Q1: Current batch = 80×10 = 800 L Q2: D=29,200; EOQ = √(2×29200×200/(10×0.15)) = √7,786,667 ≈ 2,791 L Q3: TC(800)=7,900; TC(2791)=4,183 → saves 3,717€/yr Q4: z(99%)=2.33; SS = 2.33×20×√1 = 47 L Q5: Orders/yr = 29200/800 = 36.5; stockouts = 0.01×36.5 = 0.37 → ~1 stockout every 3 years Q6: SS stays the SAME (doesn't depend on Q) Q7: ROP = 80×1 + 47 = 127 L Q8: Same ROP = 127 L (SS doesn't change with Q)

Ex 4: Emmental Cheese — Policy Audit

Part I: Sourcing Screws — Supplier Comparison (20 pts)

EOQ + SS + Periodic▸

D̄=100 boxes/day, σ=30. Review daily (R=1). i=10%, warehouse=2,000. SL=99%. US: v=500€, S=100€, transport=40€/kg (0.25kg/box), LT=7. Portugal: v=300€, S=150€, transport=30€/kg, LT=1.

US: Transport/box = 40×0.25 = 10€ (non-differential) VP = LT+R = 7+1 = 8 days SS = 2.33×30×√8 = 197 → SS cost = 197×0.1×500 = 9,850€/yr ROP = 100×8+197 = 997 EOQ = √(2×36500×100/(0.1×500)) = 382 Total = 18,250,000+365,000+9,850+9,555+9,550 = 18,643,955€/yr Portugal: Transport/box = 7.5€ (non-differential) VP = 1+1 = 2 days SS = 2.33×30×√2 = 99 → SS cost = 99×0.1×300 = 2,970€/yr ROP = 100×2+99 = 299 EOQ = √(2×36500×150/(0.1×300)) = 604 Total = 10,950,000+273,750+2,970+9,065+9,060 = 11,244,845€/yr → Portugal saves ~7.4M€/yr (unit cost difference dominates)

Part IV: Red Carpet — Strategic (30+10 pts)

Strategy▸

IV.1: Consultant's rationale? Pros/cons? IV.2: What about Miguel?

PROS of centralization: • Inventory pooling: SS drops by √25 = 5× across 25 countries (SS_pooled = z·√25·σ₁·√VP vs 25×z·σ₁·√VP) • Disintermediation: save 15% distributor margin • Economies of scale in transport, simpler upstream logistics • Ethical: removes bribery-dependent distribution CONS: • Longer/variable delivery to peripheral countries • Loss of local relationships that DRIVE demand • Healthcare systems differ by country (EU ≠ US) • Last-mile requires hospital access, not just logistics • Alienates distributors who know surgeons personally MIGUEL: Saved sales (60% recovery in 1 month) but acted unethically (fabricated export, diverted inventory).
